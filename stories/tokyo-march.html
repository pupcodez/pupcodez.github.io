Tokyo in March

2026 · Travel · Japan

Tokyo feels like a city that’s always two steps ahead — not in a loud way, but in the details. Everything is designed to move people smoothly: trains, sidewalks, convenience stores, even the quiet order of an escalator line.

My days were built around simple missions: find good coffee, walk until my legs quit, and follow whatever neighborhood energy I stumbled into — Shibuya chaos, quiet side streets, tiny ramen counters with ten seats and perfect timing.

The best moments weren’t the “big” attractions. They were the small ones: vending machines that somehow have everything, a perfectly wrapped pastry, a 7-Eleven that feels like a cheat code, and the way the city can be crowded without ever feeling messy.

One night, I ended up walking with no plan — just letting the city pull me. Neon reflections on wet pavement, music spilling out of a basement bar, and that feeling of being totally anonymous in the best possible way.

Tokyo has this rare combination: it’s futuristic, but deeply human. The whole place runs on respect — for space, for time, for each other. It made me want to bring that same energy back home.

I left with a full camera roll, a bunch of new mental systems, and a weird craving for convenience-store egg salad sandwiches. 10/10 would do again.
